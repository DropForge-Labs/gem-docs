What is an Agile Project Plan?

At DropForge we craft our project plans in Confluence using processes combined with Epics, Stories, Issues, and Bugs (most of which are contained within Jira) for project management purposes.  Our project plans are more than just timelines and deliverables, they consist of additional metadata that enables our team to operate efficiently and our customers to have greater insight into project status.

Anticipate Changes to This Document

This document contains dynamic components, widgets, and reports.  It is considered "living" and will continue to be revised throughout the project lifecycle.

Agile Process

Our hybrid agile process entails collecting tasks and organizing work estimates in a standardized workflow.  This approach utilizes concepts from both traditional Kanban and Scrum methodologies, enhancing key performance indicators (KPIs) such as time-to-market, total-cost-of-ownership, development iteration cycle.  Our processes enable developers to increase quality and efficiency, provide project managers with much greater insight into project status, and promote effective customer budget management.  As a developer becomes available to work on a new area of the project, they choose a story or task from a queue to work on.  All of the tasks are organized on a Kanban board which shows columns for statuses of work such as work in progress, needs review and done.  The developer must focus on a few issues through their completion before moving to a new task.  This crucial Kanban constraint is known as "Limiting work in progress" and helps to decrease developer ramp-up times and mitigate technical debt.  The DropForge agile process is unique in its use of a Kanban-based pull system, and Scrum originated timeboxing resulting in more flexible development iterations.  These iterations can be as short as a few days or as long as a few months.  All development is delivered incrementally as initial prototypes (MVPs) and then are refined with each iteration of development.

Iterations and Sprints

A set of obtainable story points will be selected for development at the start of a sprint.  The sprint will consist of processing each subset of the backlog until all tasks are done.  An iteration is considered a revisitation of the features in an additional sprint where bugs and other tasks are identified to refactor an earlier sprint's functionality.

Release cycle

Initially, several sprints are performed and at a point deemed acceptable by the development team.  At this time an early beta is staged to a shared development environment.  This beta version of the software is updated on a continuous basis as the product is developed.  Releases will be made on a regular basis to a Development environment or Standalone Feature Multi-dev environment and will be merged throughout the environments.  A typical scenario consists of the following:

  1. Feature -> Dev
  2. Dev -> Test
  3. Test -> Live

Prior to official launch and DNS cutover we typically only leverage Feature environments, along with Development and Test environments.  Test environments are used by both customers and us for review of release candidates.

- Andrew Rosborough, VP of Engineering, DropForge Labs

Organizing Epics, Users Stories, and Tasks and Bugs

When populating the kanban board, work is organized under Epics, User Stories, Tasks, and Bugs.  Generally, the work in the Kanban board is a set of living documents, which frequently change.  Epics describe broad features which include many Tasks, User Stories, and Bugs underneath them.  An appropriate Epic may be named after a section of the application, or a specific domain of functionality.  User Stories describe tasks the user wants to accomplish with the system.  This allows for full coverage of all business cases which are presented during discovery.  As action items come up that are not specifically covered by a full use case requiring a User Story, Tasks are created which specify the work being done.  Finally, as issues are moved through a review process Bugs are filed which get worked on to resolve the functionality of the initial issue.

Testing and Quality Assurance

Often Customers opt to use a service which takes screenshots of a set of pages on the website and compares them against former captures of the website to observe changes.  This is known as Visual Regression testing.  For sites which are heavy on the presentation layer of their content with minimal user customizations, it is recommended to rely on this automated process to catch glaring issues.

During a handoff phase, an issue collector may be enabled for the George Eastman Museum team to file QA issues for resolution by DropForge.

Maintenance and Ongoing Support

A help desk is utilized for customers with ongoing maintenance and support contracts.  Issues are typically responded to in under 24 hours and resolved within the terms of the contract.  This allows for communication via email or through the help desk's customer portal.  Key metrics are tracked and integrated into the Agile process.
